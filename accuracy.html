2.8 Accuracy

The accuracy of \(\hat{y}\) as a prediction for \(y\) depends on two quantities, which we will call the reducible error and the irreducible error. In general, \(\hat{f}\) will not be perfect estimate for \(f\), and this inaccuracy will introduce some error. This error is reducible because we can potentially improve the accuracy of \(\hat{f}\) by using the most appropriate statistical learning technique to estimate \(f\).

Even if it were possible to form a perfect estimate for \(f\), our prediction would still have some error in it! This is because \(Y\) is also a function of \(\epsilon\), which, by definition, cannot be predicted using \(X\).

Therefore, variability associated with \(\epsilon\) also affects the accuracy of our predictions. This is known as the irreducible error, because no matter how well we estimate \(f\), we cannot reduce the error introduced by \(\epsilon\).

The quantity \(\epsilon\) may contain unmeasured variables that are useful in predicting \(Y\)
